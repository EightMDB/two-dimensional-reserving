About Benktander Method

The Benktander method is an iterative technique that bridges the gap between the Chain Ladder and Bornhuetter-Ferguson methods. It applies credibility weighting iteratively, converging toward the Chain Ladder estimate while maintaining the stability benefits of the BF approach. This hybrid methodology provides a balanced solution that adapts to data maturity.

Key Concepts

Iterative Process

Applies the BF formula repeatedly, using the previous iteration's result as the expected loss for the next iteration.

Convergence

With sufficient iterations, the method converges to the Chain Ladder estimate, providing a smooth transition.

Credibility Evolution

Each iteration increases credibility given to observed data versus a priori expectations.

Flexible Credibility

Number of iterations controls the balance between BF stability and CL responsiveness.

Iterative Formula

Benktander Reserve Calculation

Iteration 0: R₀ = BF Estimate
Iteration n: Rₙ = (Expected Ultimate from Rₙ₋₁) × % Unreported

Where:

Expected Ultimate = Paid + Rₙ₋₁

Iteration Behavior:

  • 1 Iteration: Identical to Bornhuetter-Ferguson
  • 2 Iterations: Standard Benktander (GB1)
  • Many Iterations: Converges to Chain Ladder
  • Typical Use: 1-3 iterations for practical applications

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages

  • Balances stability and responsiveness
  • Smooth transition from BF to CL
  • Flexible credibility control via iterations
  • Reduces impact of outliers
  • Works well for intermediate maturities
  • Maintains mathematical consistency

Limitations

  • Still requires selection of expected loss ratio
  • More complex than single-iteration methods
  • Iteration count selection can be subjective
  • May not add value for very mature years
  • Computational overhead increases with iterations

When to Use Benktander

Ideal Applications

  • Intermediate maturity accident years
  • When you want to blend BF and CL approaches
  • Lines with moderate development volatility
  • As a sensitivity test between methods
  • When credibility should evolve over time

Consider Alternatives

  • Very immature years → Use BF directly
  • Mature years with stable patterns → Use Chain Ladder
  • When expected loss ratios are unreliable
  • Simple analyses where complexity isn't warranted
  • Lines with very rapid or very slow development

Implementation Steps

1

Establish Initial Estimate

Calculate Bornhuetter-Ferguson reserve as the starting point (Iteration 0).

2

Determine Development Patterns

Calculate age-to-age factors and percent unreported for each development period.

3

Apply First Iteration

Use BF estimate + paid as new "expected ultimate" and recalculate reserves.

4

Repeat as Needed

Continue iterating until desired credibility level or convergence is achieved.

5

Analyze Results

Compare results across iterations and with Chain Ladder to select appropriate iteration count.

Selecting Number of Iterations

1

One Iteration (BF)

Use when: Data is very immature or unreliable

Result: Identical to Bornhuetter-Ferguson

2

Two Iterations (GB1)

Use when: Moderate maturity, balanced credibility desired

Result: Standard Benktander, commonly used in practice

3-5

Multiple Iterations

Use when: Greater weight to actual data is appropriate

Result: Moves closer to Chain Ladder estimate

∞

Convergence (CL)

Use when: Data is mature and reliable

Result: Converges to Chain Ladder (just use CL directly)
