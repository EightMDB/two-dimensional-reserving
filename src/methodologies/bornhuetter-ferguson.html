About Bornhuetter-Ferguson Method

The Bornhuetter-Ferguson (BF) method combines expected loss ratios with actual development patterns to produce stable reserve estimates. This approach is particularly valuable when historical data is limited or unreliable, as it incorporates a priori expectations while still reflecting actual development experience.

Stable Estimates
Credibility Weighted
Expected Loss Based

Key Concepts

Expected Loss Ratio

A priori expectation of ultimate loss ratio based on pricing assumptions, historical experience, or expert judgment.

Percent Unreported

The proportion of ultimate losses not yet reported, calculated from development patterns.

Credibility Weighting

Balances between expected losses and actual development patterns based on data maturity.

Exposure Base

Premium or other measure used to apply expected loss ratios (earned premium, written premium, etc.).

Core Formula

Bornhuetter-Ferguson Reserve Formula

Reserve = (Expected Ultimate Loss × % Unreported) + (Actual Reported - Expected Reported)

Or equivalently:

Reserve = Expected Ultimate × (1 - % Reported) - (Paid to Date)

Component Breakdown:

  • Expected Ultimate Loss = Exposure × Expected Loss Ratio
  • % Unreported = 1 - (1 / Cumulative Development Factor to Ultimate)
  • Expected Reported = Expected Ultimate × % Reported

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages

  • More stable than Chain Ladder for immature periods
  • Incorporates external information and expertise
  • Less sensitive to random fluctuations
  • Works well with limited historical data
  • Reduces volatility in reserve estimates

Limitations

  • Requires selection of appropriate expected loss ratio
  • May not capture emerging trends quickly
  • Sensitive to choice of exposure base
  • Can be overly conservative for mature periods
  • Assumes constant expected loss ratio

When to Use Bornhuetter-Ferguson

Recommended For

  • New lines of business with limited history
  • Immature accident years with high uncertainty
  • Lines with significant environmental changes
  • When pricing data provides good benchmarks
  • Volatile lines where stability is important

Use with Caution

  • Mature accident years with stable patterns
  • When expected loss ratios are unreliable
  • Lines experiencing rapid development changes
  • When actual experience diverges from expectations
  • Short-tail business with quick settlement

Implementation Steps

1

Select Expected Loss Ratio

Determine appropriate expected loss ratio from pricing studies, historical averages, or industry benchmarks.

2

Calculate Development Patterns

Compute age-to-age development factors from historical triangles (similar to Chain Ladder).

3

Determine Percent Unreported

Calculate the percentage of ultimate losses not yet emerged for each development period.

4

Apply BF Formula

Combine expected losses with percent unreported to estimate reserves for each accident year.

5

Validate Results

Compare BF estimates with Chain Ladder and other methods. Investigate significant differences.
