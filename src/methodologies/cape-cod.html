About Cape Cod Method

The Cape Cod method, also known as the Stanard-Bühlmann method, calculates an expected loss ratio from the data itself rather than using an externally-selected ratio. It weights each accident year by its exposure and percent reported to derive an overall expected loss ratio, which is then applied back to individual years. This self-calibrating approach combines the benefits of exposure-based methods with empirical data.

Key Concepts

Implied Loss Ratio

Calculated from the data by weighting reported losses by exposure and percent reported, producing an internally consistent estimate.

Exposure Base

Premium or other measure (earned, written, or in-force premium) used to weight accident years and apply the loss ratio.

Credibility Weighting

Automatically assigns more credibility to accident years with greater exposure and development maturity.

Self-Consistency

The expected loss ratio is derived from the same data being analyzed, creating internal consistency.

Core Formulas

Cape Cod Calculation Steps

Step 1: Calculate Implied Loss Ratio

ELR = Σ(Reported Losses) / Σ(Exposure × % Reported)

Step 2: Calculate Expected Ultimate Loss

Expected Ultimate = Exposure × ELR

Step 3: Calculate Reserve

Reserve = Expected Ultimate × (1 - % Reported) - Paid

Where:

  • ELR = Expected Loss Ratio (calculated from data)
  • Exposure = Earned premium, written premium, or exposure measure
  • % Reported = 1 / (Cumulative Development Factor to Ultimate)
  • Σ = Sum across all accident years

Cape Cod vs Bornhuetter-Ferguson

Aspect Cape Cod Bornhuetter-Ferguson
Expected Loss Ratio Calculated from data itself Selected externally (pricing, judgment)
Exposure Weighting Explicitly weighted by exposure Implicit through loss ratio application
Self-Calibration Yes - internally consistent No - relies on external input
Subjectivity Lower - ratio determined by data Higher - requires ratio selection
Best Use Case Stable portfolios, varying exposures When external benchmarks available

Advantages & Limitations

Advantages

  • No need to select external expected loss ratio
  • Automatically weights by exposure and maturity
  • Handles varying premium levels across years
  • Self-calibrating and internally consistent
  • Reduces subjectivity compared to BF
  • Works well with premium growth/decline

Limitations

  • Assumes consistent loss ratio across years
  • Sensitive to unusual years in the data
  • May not capture year-specific trends
  • Requires accurate exposure data
  • Can be unstable with limited data
  • Doesn't incorporate external market information

When to Use Cape Cod

Ideal Applications

  • Portfolios with significant premium growth/decline
  • When loss ratios are relatively stable over time
  • Lines with reliable exposure measurements
  • When external loss ratio selection is difficult
  • As a check against externally-selected BF ratios
  • Mixed maturities requiring automatic weighting

Use with Caution

  • When loss ratios vary significantly by year
  • Portfolios undergoing major changes
  • Limited data (few accident years)
  • When exposure measures are unreliable
  • Presence of significant outlier years
  • Rapidly evolving underwriting or claims practices

Implementation Steps

1

Gather Exposure Data

Collect premium or other exposure measure for each accident year (earned premium is most common).

2

Calculate Development Factors

Determine age-to-age factors and percent reported using standard triangle techniques.

3

Compute Implied Loss Ratio

Calculate weighted average loss ratio using reported losses and exposure × % reported.

4

Apply to Each Year

Use implied loss ratio with each year's exposure to calculate expected ultimate losses.

5

Calculate Reserves

Determine reserves using expected ultimate losses and percent unreported for each year.

Selecting the Exposure Base

Earned Premium

Most Common Choice

✓ Matches revenue recognition

✓ Aligns with loss emergence

⚠ May lag for long-tail lines

Alternative Measure

✓ Simple and readily available

✓ Good for quickly growing books

⚠ May not align with loss periods

For Long-Tail Lines

✓ Better for extended exposure periods

✓ Captures ongoing risk

⚠ More complex to calculate

Policy Count / Other

Non-Premium Measures

✓ Useful when premium is distorted

✓ Can use claims count, units, etc.

⚠ Must be correlated with losses

Simplified Example

Cape Cod Calculation Walkthrough

AY Earned Prem Reported Loss % Reported Weighted Denom
2020 $1,000,000 $650,000 90% $900,000
2021 $1,200,000 $720,000 75% $900,000
2022 $1,500,000 $750,000 50% $750,000

Calculation:

Implied Loss Ratio = $2,120,000 / $2,550,000 = 83.1%

Expected Ultimate (2022) = $1,500,000 × 83.1% = $1,246,500

Reserve (2022) = $1,246,500 × (1 - 50%) = $623,250 (- paid portion)
